03 / Soft Robotics

Prototypes

Spider-inspired robotic leg — summer project at the MIT Media Lab @ Taipei Tech.

Last summer, I interned at City Science Lab Taipei, a research group focused on developing smart city infrastructure, autonomous vehicles, and robotics. The environment differed from the structured classes at school. Everyone in the lab seemed to know what they needed to do. Although this freedom allowed me to explore any subject I wanted, I struggled to keep up with the lab’s fast pace and felt intimidated by the complex robotics equations written by the graduate students. I couldn’t join their conversations about advanced robotics control theory. But once I started researching these concepts independently at night and asking the lab’s researchers questions during the day, I gradually started understanding terms like discrete time systems and line searches and got a better sense of the researchers’ projects.

While the lab focused on perfecting existing robotics controls using the Robot Operating System in the biped and quadruped robots, I found myself drawn to the movement of organisms in nature. I studied soft robotic actuators and shape-memory alloys and designed a robotic leg that resembles the complexity of a spider leg. This design improves maneuverability on rough terrain, making search and rescue robots more effective in real-world scenarios where soft robots can outperform traditional ones. Prototyping my designs opened my eyes to a new world of mechanical engineering.

I learned that engineering can be rigid and fluid, structured yet flexible. Watching nature’s flexibility merge with the precision of classical control systems captivated me and revealed how such combinations can create solutions that serve communities in ways traditional engineering cannot. This lab showed me what learning in higher education looks like, and taught me that the willingness to ask questions is the key to overcoming the discomfort of unfamiliar subjects. My internship also sparked my interest in soft robotics. It inspired me to develop innovative technologies in college that bridge the gap between nature and machinery, improving lives through sustainable and adaptive engineering.
